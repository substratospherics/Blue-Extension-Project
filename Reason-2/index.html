Why is blue so rare in nature?

REASON 2

Blue structural coloration, which is the result of light scattering, is rarer in nature than pigmentation-based coloration.

  • Some animals and plants use structural coloration to create different colors by changing how light reflects off of them. This differs from pigmentation-based coloration, which occurs when an animal or plant has a particular color due to pigments (such as paint) in its skin or leaves.
  • Structural coloration is a type of coloration that is produced by the physical structure of an organism, rather than by pigments. Blue structural coloration in particular is rare in nature because it requires specific structural arrangements to produce. Pigment-based coloration, on the other hand, is much more common and is produced by pigments that absorb certain wavelengths of light and reflect others. This means that blue structural coloration is less frequently found in nature than pigmentation-based coloration because it is more difficult to produce and maintain.
  • The blue color of certain butterfly wings is an example of structural coloration. The blue color is caused by how tiny structures on the wing surface scatter blue light in a specific way, not by pigments.
  • Because structural coloration is more difficult to produce, it is more rare in nature than pigmentation-based coloration. The development of complex structures, such as the scales of a butterfly wing, is often required to create structural coloration, whereas pigmentation-based coloration can be created simply by adding pigments.
  • Pigmentation coloration can be thought of as painting a wall with different colors of paint, where the color comes from pigments present in the paint. Structural coloration, on the other hand, is similar to building a wall with variously shaped bricks that are arranged in a specific pattern to create different colors. The color in this case comes from the way light interacts with the structure of the bricks. Structural coloration is a rare and more complex process than pigmentation as it involves the manipulation of the physical structure to produce color, rather than relying on pigments.
