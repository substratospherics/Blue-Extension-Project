Why is blue so rare in nature?

REASON 3

Many blue animals have structural features that give off a blue iridescence rather than being truly blue.

  • Many animals that appear blue, such as birds, fish, and insects, actually have structural features that convey a blue iridescence rather than being truly blue. This is referred to as "structural coloration." Structural coloration is caused by an object's physical properties rather than pigments, which are the basis of pigmentary coloration.
  • Iridescence is when an object appears to change color when viewed from different angles. It's like when a soap bubble changes color in the light or when a CD reflects different colors in the light.
  • The blue color in blue structural coloration is caused by how light interacts with the surface of the animal's feathers, scales, or exoskeleton. These structures' surfaces are made up of layers of cells or scales stacked in such a way that they interfere with light in a specific way. Because of this interference, certain wavelengths of light are reflected more strongly than others, resulting in a blue iridescence.
  • So, these animals have special pigments or structures in their feathers, scales, or wings that make them look blue, but this color is not coming from a blue pigment, it's coming from the way the light is reflecting off of these special structures. The color changes as the angle of light or the angle you're looking changes.
  • The structural characteristics that produce this blue iridescence can be quite complex. The interaction of light with the microscopic structure of some birds' feathers, for example, produces the blue color. The feathers of these birds are made up of layers that are stacked on top of one another, each with a different refractive index. When light strikes the feather, it interacts with these layers, causing certain wavelengths of light to be reflected more strongly than others and giving the feather its blue color.
  • The interaction of light with the surface of other animals' scales or exoskeletons produces the blue color. The surface of these scales or exoskeletons has a series of grooves or ridges that diffract light in such a way that certain wavelengths of light are reflected more strongly than others, resulting in the blue color.
  • It is important to note that the iridescent blue color is a result of the way light interacts with these structural features, and that it can change depending on the angle of the light, and angle of observation.
