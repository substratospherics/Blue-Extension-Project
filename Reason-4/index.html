Why is blue so rare in nature?

REASON 4

Blue is less common in minerals and crystals than other colors.

  • Blue is less common in minerals and crystals than other colors because minerals and crystals with the right chemical composition and structure to produce blue are more rare.
  • Minerals and crystals are composed of various elements and compounds, and the color they exhibit is frequently determined by the specific chemical composition and the arrangement of the atoms in the crystal structure. Different colors are produced by different types of electron transitions and light wave absorption. Blue color is produced by a specific set of conditions, such as a specific type of crystal structure and the presence of specific elements or impurities.
  • To put it simply, minerals and crystals are like puzzle pieces; they must have the correct shape and number of pieces in order to form a specific color, in this case blue. Other colors, such as red, yellow, and green, are more common and easier to form because they do not necessitate the same conditions as blue.
  • To elaborate, the presence of specific elements, the precise arrangement of atoms, and the precise size and shape of the crystal are all required for blue structural coloration in minerals and crystals. The mineral lazurite, for example, is known for its deep blue color, which is caused by the presence of sulfur and titanium atoms arranged in a specific pattern. Similarly, the presence of titanium and iron atoms in a specific crystal structure gives blue sapphires their color. The precise conditions required for these structural colorations to occur make blue minerals and crystals more rare.
